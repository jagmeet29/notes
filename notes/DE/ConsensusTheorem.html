Proof of the Consensus Theorem

The Consensus Theorem states that:

$A \cdot B + \bar{A} \cdot C + B \cdot C = A \cdot B + \bar{A} \cdot C$

This theorem essentially claims that the term $B \cdot C$ is redundant. Let's prove this using algebraic manipulation.

Algebraic Proof

Starting with the left side of the equation:

$A \cdot B + \bar{A} \cdot C + B \cdot C$

Step 1: Expand $B \cdot C$ using the identity property

Since $A + \bar{A} = 1$ for any Boolean variable (law of the excluded middle), we can write:

$B \cdot C = B \cdot C \cdot (A + \bar{A})$

Step 2: Apply the distributive law

$B \cdot C \cdot (A + \bar{A}) = B \cdot C \cdot A + B \cdot C \cdot \bar{A} = A \cdot B \cdot C + \bar{A} \cdot B \cdot C$

Step 3: Substitute back into the original expression

$A \cdot B + \bar{A} \cdot C + B \cdot C = A \cdot B + \bar{A} \cdot C + A \cdot B \cdot C + \bar{A} \cdot B \cdot C$

Step 4: Apply the absorption law ($X + X \cdot Y = X$)

  • $A \cdot B + A \cdot B \cdot C = A \cdot B$ (since $A \cdot B$ absorbs $A \cdot B \cdot C$)
  • $\bar{A} \cdot C + \bar{A} \cdot B \cdot C = \bar{A} \cdot C$ (since $\bar{A} \cdot C$ absorbs $\bar{A} \cdot B \cdot C$)

Step 5: Combine results

$A \cdot B + \bar{A} \cdot C + A \cdot B \cdot C + \bar{A} \cdot B \cdot C = A \cdot B + \bar{A} \cdot C$

Thus:

$A \cdot B + \bar{A} \cdot C + B \cdot C = A \cdot B + \bar{A} \cdot C$
